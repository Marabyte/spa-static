Uranus

Uranus is the third largest planet in the Solar System and the second furthest from the Sun. While most planets spin like tops on an axis, Uranus spins on its side like a rolling ball. Scientists believe that a major collision with an object much larger than an asteroid caused the planet to tip over. Uranus is named after the ancient Greek god of the sky. It is very hard to see from Earth with the naked eye, and it was the first planet to be discovered using a telescope.

The amount of sunlight that Uranus receives is only 0.25 percent of that reaching Earth.

In comparison to other gas planets, such as Jupiter and Saturn, Uranus has a relatively calm atmosphere. As a result, its surface appears uniform. Small amounts of the chemical methane in the planet’s atmosphere give Uranus its blue color.

Uranus, like the other three gas planets, has its own ring system. It is surrounded by 13 narrow rings made of chunks of dark material. Seen from Earth, the very faint rings of Uranus appear to encircle the planet from top to bottom.

Uranus lies on its side as it orbits the Sun, and each of the poles points to the Sun for 21 years at a time. This means that while one pole spends many years in continuous sunlight, the other pole will be in complete darkness.

The faint white bands seen on the surface of Uranus are wisps of clouds at the topmost layer of its atmosphere.
